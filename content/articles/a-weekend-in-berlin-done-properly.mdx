Berlin is not a city that tries to impress you. It doesn't need to. The moment you step off the U-Bahn at Warschauer Straße on a Friday night and see the queue snaking around the corner of a concrete power station, you understand — this city operates on its own terms.

## Friday Night: Ease In

Don't rush to the clubs. Berlin rewards patience. Start with drinks somewhere low-key in Neukölln or Kreuzberg. The bars here don't close early, and nobody is in a hurry.

<Callout type="tip">
If you're arriving on a Friday, resist the urge to go straight to Berghain. Rest up. Saturday night into Sunday morning is when the magic happens.
</Callout>

Find a neighbourhood bar, talk to strangers, get a feel for the city's rhythm. Berlin is one of the few places where the night genuinely doesn't end — so you can afford to take your time.

## Saturday: The Long Day

Saturday is about Schöneberg and the old-school gay scene. Wander Motzstraße in the afternoon — it's been the heart of gay Berlin since the Weimar Republic. The cafés and bars here have a different energy to Kreuzberg: older, more settled, unapologetically itself.

In the evening, things shift. Head to Kreuzberg or Friedrichshain. This is when the city starts to move.

### Where to Go

The options depend on what you're after:

- **Schwuz** — Berlin's biggest queer club. Multiple floors, different music on each, and a crowd that spans the entire spectrum. Great for a first night out.
- **SchwuZ** runs regular themed nights — check what's on before you go.
- **Roses** — A tiny, chaotic Kreuzberg bar covered floor-to-ceiling in kitsch. It's not a club, it's an experience. Go after midnight.

## Sunday: The Main Event

This is what you came for.

<Callout type="info">
Berghain typically opens on Saturday night and runs through to Monday morning. Sunday afternoon is when the energy peaks — the tourists have gone home and the floor belongs to the people who were always going to be there.
</Callout>

There's no trick to getting in. Be yourself, know what's on, don't come in a group of ten. The door policy isn't about exclusivity — it's about protecting a space where people can be completely free.

## The Unwritten Rules

1. **No phones on the dance floor.** Stickers go over your camera at the door. This isn't performative — it's how the space stays safe.
2. **Don't overdress.** Berlin's dress code is no dress code. Black is fine. Streetwear is fine. Trying too hard is the only wrong answer.
3. **Eat before you go.** There's a reason every kebab shop near Berghain is open at 6am.

---

Berlin doesn't sell itself as a gay destination. It just is one — in the most effortless, uncompromising way possible. A weekend here isn't a holiday. It's a reminder of what nightlife can be when nobody is watching.
